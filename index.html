The Great Barrier Reef

Facts

Coral Bleaching: ~60%
Types of Species: 1,500+
Reef Area: 344,000 km²
Overview

The Great Barrier Reef is the largest living structure in the world. It expands as big as 344,000 km2. Not only that, it holds the record of the largest coral reef in the world. Many fish live in the Great Barrier Reef such as 100 species of jellyfish, 3000 types of mollusks, 1625 types of fish, 133 varieties of sharks and rays, and 30+ species of whales and dolphins. However, the Great Barrier Reef is very vulnerable and sensitive.

Due to the sensitivity of the reef, many tourists aren't allowed to visit the reef. Climate change is a major factor in the growth of the reef. Over 95% of Coral Bleaching has occurred since 1995.

Effects of Climate Change

The Great Barrier reef has lost almost 50 percent of it's corals since 1985 due to climate change. At this rate, there will be no more "Great Barrier Reef" by 2050. According to Linwood et al. (2019), "results from laboratory, field and model studies converge to show that we can expect OA (Oceanic Acidification), particularly in combination with warming, to cause major changes in GBR (Great Barrier Reef) communities, including loss of reef framework, biodiversity and ecosystem services. While warming remains the most 'acute concern for the GBR, with mass bleaching events expected to continue in the years to come." Based off of this evidence, we can expect that climate change is ruining the reef throught its Oceanic Acidification (Coral Bleaching).

Coral Reefs play a giant role in our ocean'ss ecosystem by serving as a guard, home, and food rich for species living there. Not only that, it protects the land surrounding the reef by minimizing waves/tropical storms. According to the Natural History Museum, "More than 500 million people worldwide depend on reefs for food, jobs and coastal defence. The ridges in coral reefs act as barriers and can reduce wave energy by up to 97%, providing crucial protection from threats such as tsunamis" (Natural Museum Of History, 2021).

Environmental and Social Effects

WFF stated that, "pollution has caused deadly starfish outbreaks, and global warming has produced horrific coral bleaching" (WWF Austrailia). The Great Barrier Reef has affected other ecosystems due to it's high pollution levels, leading to massive and deadly starfish outbreaks. These olutbreaks allow for many endangered species to go extinct. Plus, the consistent pollution affects the reef due to it getting latched or eaten by fish. All of these effects dive into other important ecosystems under and over water leading to their destruction. This can be prevented through net-zero emmissions, less pollution, and more care!

Conclusion

The Great Barrier Reef is a very important part of our earth (especially Australia). However, due to the rapid increase in global warming, CNN states, "But on closer examination, the story loses some of its beauty. The reef -- along with the multibillion-dollar tourist industry it supports -- could be extinct by 2050" (Abitbol, 2017). It would be a great tragedy to lose such an important and beautiful reef. From Georges Casassovici's experience, he'll definitely be sad to not be able to visit the sea wildlife there.
